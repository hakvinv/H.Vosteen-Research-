\noindent{\LARGE\textbf{Burnout Is Not Too Much Work, v2}}\\[0.3em]
\noindent{\small Hakvin Vosteen \hfill 2026}
\vspace{0.3cm}

\noindent{\small\textit{Theoretical model. Population calibration illustrative.}}
\vspace{0.7cm}

\noindent The v1 post had three holes. A reader pointed them out. The threshold is
not universal, it is individual. ``Sustained period'' is not a definition of time.
And the overhead/output split is observer-dependent, because maintaining a facade
counts as productive output for the firm and as overhead for the person doing it.
This is the v2 that fixes all three.

\medskip\noindent The punchline up front. With realistic numbers, a person at
$\rho^{P} = 1.50$ (collapse-imminent) shows up on the firm's dashboard as
$\rho^{F} = 0.54$ (green). Same hour of work, two valid bookkeepings, almost
a full unit apart. Algebra below.

\end{center}

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Setup.}\quad Let $i$ index people and $t \geq 0$ index time.
Each person carries a private friction-output ratio $\rho_i(t)$ and a private
threshold $\rho_i^*$. Burnout is the event $\rho_i(t) > \rho_i^*$. The right tail
of the distribution is the burned-out fraction.

\newpage

% =============== PAGE 2 ===============
\noindent The first fix is that the output category is observer-dependent. The
firm pays for outcomes. Some of those outcomes are facade. The person delivers
the facade but receives no internal value from it. So the same hour of work
gets booked differently by two accountants.

\begin{definition}[Observer-Relative Energy Split]
For a working hour, decompose total energy as $E_{\text{tot}} = E_{\text{out}} + E_{\text{ovr}}$.
For person $i$, write
\[
E_{\text{out},i}^{P}, \quad E_{\text{ovr},i}^{P} \quad \text{(person's accounting)},
\]
\[
E_{\text{out},i}^{F}, \quad E_{\text{ovr},i}^{F} \quad \text{(firm's accounting)}.
\]
Define the role-coupling set
$\mathcal{F}_i = \{ \text{tasks the person does not value intrinsically but the firm pays for} \}$.
Then $E_{\text{out},i}^{F} = E_{\text{out},i}^{P} + E_{\mathcal{F},i}$ and
$E_{\text{ovr},i}^{F} = E_{\text{ovr},i}^{P} - E_{\mathcal{F},i}$.
\end{definition}

\noindent The set $\mathcal{F}_i$ is the wedge between two valid accountings.

\begin{theorem}[Observer-Relative Burnout Inequality]
Define the two ratios $\rho_i^{P}(t) = E_{\text{ovr},i}^{P}/E_{\text{out},i}^{P}$
and $\rho_i^{F}(t) = E_{\text{ovr},i}^{F}/E_{\text{out},i}^{F}$. Then
\[
\rho_i^{P}(t) \;\geq\; \rho_i^{F}(t),
\]
with equality if and only if $E_{\mathcal{F},i} = 0$, i.e., the role contains
no task the person does facade-only.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Write $a = E_{\text{ovr},i}^{P}$, $b = E_{\text{out},i}^{P}$, $f = E_{\mathcal{F},i}$,
all nonnegative with $b > 0$. By the definition above, $\rho_i^{P} = a/b$ and
$\rho_i^{F} = (a-f)/(b+f)$. Subtracting,
\begin{align}
\rho_i^{P} - \rho_i^{F}
&= \frac{a}{b} - \frac{a-f}{b+f} \\
&= \frac{a(b+f) - b(a-f)}{b(b+f)} \\
&= \frac{f(a+b)}{b(b+f)}.
\end{align}
Since $a, b, f \geq 0$ and $b > 0$, the numerator and denominator are
nonnegative, so $\rho_i^{P} - \rho_i^{F} \geq 0$. The expression is zero
exactly when $f = 0$.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}
The person can collapse ($\rho_i^{P} > \rho_i^{*}$) while the firm metric
$\rho_i^{F}$ still reads healthy. The larger the facade load $E_{\mathcal{F},i}$,
the larger the gap.
\end{corollary}

\noindent This is the ``high performer suddenly burns out'' pattern. The firm
sees a productive employee until the day they stop functioning. The person was
already past threshold for weeks. The two accountings disagreed.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Worked example.}\quad Take a knowledge-worker hour with
$a = 60$ (person-counted overhead, including facade), $b = 40$ (work the person
values), $f = 25$ (facade portion of the overhead). Then
\begin{align}
\rho^{P} &= \frac{60}{40} = 1.50 \quad \text{(person, past collapse threshold)} \\
\rho^{F} &= \frac{60 - 25}{40 + 25} = \frac{35}{65} = 0.54 \quad \text{(firm, looks fine)} \\
\rho^{P} - \rho^{F} &= \frac{25 \cdot 100}{40 \cdot 65} = 0.96.
\end{align}
Almost a full unit of disagreement on the same hour of work.

% =============== PAGE 3 ===============
\noindent The second fix is making time explicit. v1 said ``sustained period.''
That is not a clock. We need a generator for $\rho_i(t)$.

\begin{definition}[Friction Drift]
Let $\rho_i(t)$ evolve according to
\[
\frac{d \rho_i}{dt} = \alpha_i(\text{env}) - \beta_i \cdot r_i(t),
\]
where $\alpha_i \geq 0$ is the environmental overhead-injection rate
(meetings, interrupts, facade demand), $\beta_i \geq 0$ is the per-person
restoration sensitivity, and $r_i(t) \in [0,1]$ is the actual recovery
intensity at time $t$ (sleep, weekends, social repair).
\end{definition}

\begin{center}
\includegraphics[width=0.78\textwidth]{figures/observer_split.pdf}
\end{center}

\noindent Same job, two accountings. The dark bar is what the person spends
their day doing. The light bar is what the firm sees on the timesheet. The
facade column is invisible to the firm because it is folded into ``task
execution'' on payroll. To the person it is its own column, costing 25\% of
the energy budget.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Steady state.}\quad Setting $d\rho_i/dt = 0$ in
Definition~2 with mean recovery $\bar r_i$ gives
\[
\rho_i^{ss} = \frac{\alpha_i}{\beta_i \bar r_i}.
\]
The person settles at this ratio if nothing changes. Burnout is the condition
$\rho_i^{ss} > \rho_i^{*}$. The relevant time scale is the relaxation time
$\tau_i \approx 1/(\beta_i \bar r_i)$, which sets how fast $\rho_i$ approaches
its steady state after a perturbation.

\begin{remark}[Why ``rest'' often fails]
A sabbatical raises $r_i(t)$ to nearly 1 for a few weeks, pulling $\rho_i$ down
with rate $\beta_i$. But $\alpha_i(\text{env})$ is unchanged. On return,
$\rho_i$ relaxes back to the same $\rho_i^{ss}$ over time $\tau_i$. Empirically
this matches the $\sim 50\%$ recurrence within 6 months reported by Maslach
and Leiter (2016), implying $\tau_i \sim$ several months. Rest moves you down
the curve. Only changing $\alpha_i$ (the environment) changes where the curve
ends up.
\end{remark}

\newpage

% =============== PAGE 4 ===============
\noindent The third fix is making the population explicit.
The threshold $\rho_i^*$ is private and varies between people. So is the drift
rate. Aggregating gives a population curve.

\begin{theorem}[Population Burnout Curve]
Let $\log \rho_i(t) = \mu_0 + \delta \cdot t + \varepsilon_i$ with
$\varepsilon_i \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2)$, and let
$\log \rho_i^* \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu^*, \sigma_*^2)$, independent of $\varepsilon_i$.
Then the population fraction in burnout at time $t$ is
\[
B(t) \;=\; \Pr\!\left(\rho_i(t) > \rho_i^*\right)
\;=\; \Phi\!\left( \frac{\mu_0 + \delta t - \mu^*}{\sqrt{\sigma^2 + \sigma_*^2}} \right),
\]
where $\Phi$ is the standard normal CDF.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The event $\rho_i(t) > \rho_i^*$ is equivalent to
$\log \rho_i(t) - \log \rho_i^* > 0$. Let
$Z_i = \log \rho_i(t) - \log \rho_i^*$. By the assumptions and independence,
\begin{align}
Z_i &\sim \mathcal{N}\!\left( \mu_0 + \delta t - \mu^*, \; \sigma^2 + \sigma_*^2 \right).
\end{align}
Then $\Pr(Z_i > 0) = 1 - \Phi(-(\mu_0 + \delta t - \mu^*)/\sqrt{\sigma^2 + \sigma_*^2})
= \Phi((\mu_0 + \delta t - \mu^*)/\sqrt{\sigma^2 + \sigma_*^2})$.
\end{proof}

\begin{center}
\includegraphics[width=0.78\textwidth]{figures/b_of_t.pdf}
\end{center}

\noindent The Gallup point sits within the curve at $t \approx 88$ weeks, which
matches the average tenure-in-current-role for the affected demographic. This
is calibration consistency, not validation.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{The argument.}\quad Three fixes, one equation:

\[
  \boxed{ \; B(t) \;=\; \Phi\!\left( \frac{\mu_0 + \delta t - \mu^*}{\sqrt{\sigma^2 + \sigma_*^2}} \right) \quad \text{with} \quad \rho_i^{P} \geq \rho_i^{F} \; }
\]

\noindent Burnout is a population sigmoid in time, driven by individual drift,
gated by individual thresholds, and obscured by an observer-dependent
accounting wedge.

% =============== PAGE 5 ===============
\medskip\noindent\textbf{What happens when.}\quad The sigmoid has four regimes,
each with a different prediction.

\begin{tabular}{llll}
\toprule
Phase & Condition & $B(t)$ & In our calibration \\
\midrule
Honeymoon  & $\mu_0 + \delta t \ll \mu^*$ & $\approx 0$ & $t < 20$ wk \\
Climb      & $\mu_0 + \delta t < \mu^*$   & rising      & $20$--$50$ wk \\
Inflection & $\mu_0 + \delta t = \mu^*$   & $0.5$       & $t \approx 58$ wk \\
Saturation & $\mu_0 + \delta t \gg \mu^*$ & $\to 1$     & $t > 100$ wk \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}

\medskip\noindent The slope at the inflection point gives the worst-case rate
of new burnouts:
\[
\left.\frac{dB}{dt}\right|_{B=0.5} = \frac{\delta}{\sqrt{2\pi(\sigma^2 + \sigma_*^2)}} \approx 0.9\%/\text{wk}.
\]
At peak rate, roughly 1\% of the cohort flips into burnout per week. This is the
window where intervention has the highest marginal return.

\begin{tabular}{lll}

\vspace{0.5cm}
\noindent\textbf{What this model still does not address}

\noindent Depression as an independent contributor that lowers $E_{\text{tot}}$.
Pure physical exhaustion. Burnout-by-meaninglessness, where output is high but
its perceived value is zero (this would require a separate term modulating
$E_{\text{out},i}^{P}$). Inter-person spillover, where one person's $\rho_i$
raises another's $\alpha_j$. The model is the skeleton, not the body.
