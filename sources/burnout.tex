\noindent{\LARGE\textbf{Burnout Is Not Too Much Work, v3}}\\[0.3em]
\noindent{\small Hakvin Vosteen, the new-job decay edition \hfill 2026}
\vspace{0.3cm}

\noindent{\small\textit{Theoretical model. Decay constants illustrative.}}
\vspace{0.7cm}

\noindent v2 explained why rest fails. It did not explain why a brand-new job
feels different at month six. Same hours, same desk, same colleagues, less will
to start the day. The numerator did not change. The denominator did. v3 adds
the missing term: hedonic decay of perceived output value. The ratio rises from
below, even when nothing about the job objectively got worse.

\medskip\noindent The punchline up front. A new job at $\rho_0 = 0.6$ (well in
the safe zone) drifts to $\rho^P = 1.5$ over twelve months purely because the
pride-and-novelty multiplier collapses from 1 to 0.4. No extra meetings. No
extra interrupts. The same hour of work just stops counting for as much.

\begin{center}
\includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{figures/hook_decay.pdf}
\end{center}

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Setup.}\quad Define $E_{\text{out},i}^{P}(t)$ as
\textit{perceived} productive output for person $i$ at time $t$. The same
physical hour can be experienced as more or less productive depending on
novelty, status, learning rate, and visible wins. We capture this with a
multiplier on baseline output capacity.

\newpage

% =============== PAGE 2 ===============
\begin{definition}[Hedonic Multiplier]
For person $i$ in role-state at time $t \geq 0$, write
\[
E_{\text{out},i}^{P}(t) \;=\; m_i(t) \cdot E_{\text{out},i}^{\text{baseline}},
\]
where $E_{\text{out},i}^{\text{baseline}} > 0$ is the physical output capacity
and $m_i(t) \in (0, 1]$ is the hedonic multiplier. We assume an exponential
decay form
\[
m_i(t) \;=\; m_{\infty,i} + (1 - m_{\infty,i}) \cdot e^{-t/\tau_{m,i}},
\]
with floor $m_{\infty,i} \in (0, 1]$ and decay constant $\tau_{m,i} > 0$.
The floor encodes person-job match: high $m_\infty$ means the role keeps
generating felt value indefinitely; low $m_\infty$ means novelty was carrying
most of the weight.
\end{definition}

\noindent The functional form is the simplest one consistent with two
constraints: (i) $m(0) = 1$ at the start of the role, and (ii) $m(t) \to
m_\infty$ as $t \to \infty$. This is the standard form for hedonic adaptation
in well-being research.

\begin{theorem}[Honeymoon-Decay Climb]
Let $\rho_i^{P}(t) = E_{\text{ovr},i}^{P}/E_{\text{out},i}^{P}(t)$, and assume
$E_{\text{ovr},i}^{P}$ and $E_{\text{out},i}^{\text{baseline}}$ are constant
in $t$. Then
\[
\rho_i^{P}(t) \;=\; \frac{\rho_{0,i}}{m_i(t)}, \qquad \rho_{0,i} \;:=\; \frac{E_{\text{ovr},i}^{P}}{E_{\text{out},i}^{\text{baseline}}}.
\]
The function $\rho_i^{P}(t)$ is strictly increasing in $t$ whenever $m_{\infty,i} < 1$,
with limits $\rho_i^{P}(0) = \rho_{0,i}$ and $\rho_i^{P}(t) \to \rho_{0,i}/m_{\infty,i}$
as $t \to \infty$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The first equality is immediate from the definitions. For monotonicity,
differentiate:
\begin{align}
\frac{d \rho_i^{P}}{dt}
&= \rho_{0,i} \cdot \left( -\frac{m_i'(t)}{m_i(t)^2} \right) \\
&= \rho_{0,i} \cdot \frac{(1 - m_{\infty,i})\, e^{-t/\tau_{m,i}}}{\tau_{m,i} \cdot m_i(t)^2}.
\end{align}
The factor $\rho_{0,i} > 0$ by construction (positive overhead, positive output
capacity). The numerator $(1 - m_{\infty,i}) e^{-t/\tau_{m,i}}$ is positive
exactly when $m_{\infty,i} < 1$. The denominator $\tau_{m,i} m_i(t)^2 > 0$ always.
So $d\rho_i^{P}/dt > 0$ on $[0, \infty)$ whenever $m_{\infty,i} < 1$, i.e., the
match is imperfect. The limits follow from $m_i(0) = 1$ and $m_i(t) \to m_{\infty,i}$.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Burnout Without Drift or Wedge]
If $\rho_{0,i} > m_{\infty,i} \cdot \rho_i^{*}$, then person $i$ enters
sustained burnout at some finite $t^{*}$, even when $E_{\text{ovr},i}^{P}$ is
constant (no v2 environmental drift) and $E_{\mathcal{F},i} = 0$ (no v2 facade
wedge). The honeymoon-decay mechanism alone is sufficient.
\end{corollary}

\noindent The corollary is the key claim. v1 needed a high baseline $\rho_0$.
v2 needed environmental drift or a facade wedge. v3 needs neither. A perfectly
healthy starting ratio with a perfectly stable environment can still produce
burnout, purely from the denominator collapsing.

\noindent The lifecycle has three phases, with cleanly separated time scales.

\begin{center}
\includegraphics[width=0.78\textwidth]{figures/phase_progression.pdf}
\end{center}

\medskip
\begin{tabular}{lll}
\toprule
Phase & Range & What is happening \\
\midrule
Honeymoon       & $0 \leq t < \tau_m$       & $m \approx 1$, ratio near baseline \\
Disillusionment & $\tau_m \leq t < 3\tau_m$ & $m$ collapsing fastest, ratio climbing \\
Steady state    & $t \geq 3\tau_m$          & $m \approx m_\infty$, ratio at $\rho_0/m_\infty$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Why ``the new job wears off'' is not weakness.}\quad
The Disillusionment phase is not a moral failing. It is a prediction of any
model in which subjective output is a function of novelty and the novelty
decays. The mistake is interpreting the felt change as a signal that something
is wrong with you. The signal is correct, but it is reporting on $m_i(t)$, not
on your character.

\begin{remark}[Why job-hopping helps temporarily]
Switching roles resets $m_i$ to 1 by construction (the new role is novel by
definition). It does not change $\alpha_i(\text{env})$ from v2 or
$\rho_{0,i}$ from v3. This predicts the empirical pattern: each new job
delivers a fresh Honeymoon, but if the underlying $\rho_{0,i}/m_{\infty,i}$ is
above threshold across all available jobs, the climb repeats every cycle.
Job-hopping is rational hedonic management, not avoidance.
\end{remark}

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Estimating the constants.}\quad The decay constant
$\tau_{m,i}$ is the time over which roughly $63\%$ of the novelty premium
decays ($1 - 1/e$). For knowledge work the empirical anchor is the Gallup
engagement curve, which reports a peak in the first six months and a stable
floor reached around eighteen months. This implies $\tau_m \approx 4$ to $6$
months, consistent with the figures used here.

% =============== PAGE 4 ===============
\noindent The combined model. v1, v2, v3 are not competing explanations. They
are three independent terms.

\begin{center}
\includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{figures/mechanism_stack.pdf}
\end{center}

\medskip\noindent The static v1 line is flat because v1 had no time term at
all, only a static threshold. v2 adds environmental drift (the dashed line):
overhead grows over time, mostly linear in log space. v3 adds hedonic decay
(the dash-dot line): denominator collapses on its own time scale. The combined
trajectory crosses threshold earliest. In our calibration v2 alone takes
$22$ months to cross, v3 alone takes $7$ months, both together take $5$ months.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{The full equation.}\quad Allowing both mechanisms,
\[
\boxed{ \; \rho_i^{P}(t) \;=\; \frac{E_{\text{ovr},i}^{P}(t)}{m_i(t) \cdot E_{\text{out},i}^{\text{baseline}}}, \qquad
\frac{d \log \rho_i^{P}}{dt} \;=\; \underbrace{\alpha_i(\text{env})}_{\text{v2 drift}} \;+\; \underbrace{\left|\frac{d \log m_i}{dt}\right|}_{\text{v3 decay}}. \; }
\]

\noindent The two contributions add in log space. They are individually positive
and cannot cancel. This is the structural reason that ``either reduce overhead
or take a sabbatical'' is incomplete advice: it misses the third lever, which
is increasing $m_\infty$ via better person-job match.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Three levers, three populations.}

\begin{tabular}{lll}
\toprule
Lever & Mechanism & Right intervention \\
\midrule
Lower $E_{\text{ovr}}$    & v2 (drift) & Consolidate meetings, batch interrupts \\
Reduce facade $E_{\mathcal{F}}$ & v2 (wedge) & Change role or environment \\
Raise $m_\infty$          & v3 (decay) & Better person-job match, intrinsic alignment \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}

\medskip\noindent The third row is the one that gets called ``life advice.''
It is the only one that addresses the denominator. The first two address the
numerator. Burnout interventions that focus only on the numerator and ignore
$m_\infty$ are addressing two-thirds of the problem.

% =============== PAGE 5 ===============
\medskip\noindent\textbf{Empirical anchors.}

\begin{tabular}{lll}
\toprule
Quantity & Value & Source \\
\midrule
Engagement peak after start of role     & $\sim 6$ months    & Gallup engagement series \\
Engagement floor reached                & $\sim 18$ months   & Gallup engagement series \\
Average tenure (US, all sectors, 2024)  & $\sim 4.1$ years   & BLS \\
Average tenure for workers under 35     & $\sim 2.7$ years   & BLS \\
Hedonic adaptation half-life (life events) & $\sim 3$--$12$ months & Lyubomirsky (2011) \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}

\medskip\noindent The under-35 tenure number is the diagnostic. If the
v3 mechanism dominates, expected tenure approaches $3\tau_m$ (the Steady-state
onset), which is around $1$ to $1.5$ years. The observed $2.7$ years is
consistent with a mix of v3 (people leaving when $\rho$ becomes uncomfortable) and
non-burnout factors (compensation, location, relationships). v3 cannot explain
all job-hopping, but it predicts a robust floor below which tenures should not
fall easily; that floor matches the data.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Falsification routes.}

\noindent\textit{Route 1.}\quad Measure self-reported $m_i(t)$ in a longitudinal
panel of new hires. The model predicts an exponential decay shape with
person-job-match correlated with $m_\infty$. If decay is linear or sigmoidal,
the multiplier form is wrong.

\noindent\textit{Route 2.}\quad The model predicts that two people with
identical $\rho_{0,i}$ but different $m_{\infty,i}$ will hit threshold at
different times in the same role. Match-quality should predict tenure
controlling for compensation. If it does not, the floor is irrelevant.

\noindent\textit{Route 3.}\quad For job-switchers, $\rho^{P}$ should reset
toward $\rho_{0}$ on each transition. Wearables and engagement-survey data
should show this discontinuity. If the trajectory is smooth across transitions,
the multiplier is not actually resetting.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{What this still does not address.}\quad Hedonic
adaptation is not the only thing that scales perceived output. Status changes,
relational quality, and personal life events all modulate $E_{\text{out}}^{P}$
through routes that are not novelty-driven. The model collapses everything
that is not environmental drift or facade into a single decay constant. That is
useful for tractability and incomplete for full description.
