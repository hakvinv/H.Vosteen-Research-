Abstract

Pharmaceutical chemists have a name for it: Magic Methyl. Adding a single CH3 group at exactly the right atom in a drug candidate sometimes increases binding affinity by three orders of magnitude, while at every other position the same modification does almost nothing. We formalize the phenomenon via a Boltzmann bridge equation relating the binding free-energy change to the ratio of partition functions of accessible bound microstates, and identify Sensitivity Sites as positions where this ratio is finite. An information-theoretic reading recasts the bridge as ΔΔG = −kT · I_conformational, where I is the mutual information between methyl presence and bound conformation. The framework explains why rational prediction is hard (the relevant quantity is conformational, not chemical) and suggests AlphaFold-Multimer plus FEP scanning as a tractable attack.
