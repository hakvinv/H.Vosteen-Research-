Abstract

v4 — how to mathematical ragequit your job. v1–v3 were diagnosis (ratio, drift, hedonic decay). v4 is the action item: given everything we now know, when exactly should you quit? The answer has a name — Charnov's Marginal Value Theorem (1976), originally for birds foraging in a patch. A forager should leave the current patch when its current intake rate equals the long-run average rate over the cycle of search-plus-forage. The same equation governs jobs: quit when u(t*) = A(t*), where u(t) is current flow utility from the job and A(t*) is the long-run average over the cycle including the cost τ_s and disutility u_s of searching for a new job. Under realistic parameters, t* ≈ 9.1 months. A bird with no mortgage solves this in seconds; humans take years because τ_s and u_s are large and partly inferred from priors. The framework gives comparative statics — when should mortgages, dependents, or industry-cycle priors push t* later or earlier — and clean conditions under which the optimal stopping time exists.
