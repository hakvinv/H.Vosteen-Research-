Abstract

A math tier list posted to TikTok ran for two days and pulled comments. Of seven substantive responses, five were identity-positioning rather than ranking discussion. We formalize the observation: for any tier list T constructed by a single author drawn from a population with ≥4 subdisciplines, and at least one item whose importance varies across subdisciplines, the mutual information I(T;A) about the author's identity exceeds I(T;Θ) about ground-truth importance, provided within-subdiscipline tier assignments are more consistent than between-subdiscipline ones. The structure looks objective. The signal is identity broadcast and audience self-sorting. Boundary: the result fails inside a single subdiscipline or for consensus-objective domains.
